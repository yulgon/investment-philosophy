# Beyond the Metaphor of the Money Vessel: Why We Must Train Our 'Money Muscle'

![Money Muscle: Smashing the Bowl](/breaking_money_vessel.png)

> *"I refuse to confine myself within a ceramic vessel baked by someone else. Wealth is not a stagnant container, but a muscle that tears and pumps every single day."*

---

If you stroll through the personal finance or self-help section of any bookstore, there is one phrase you'll encounter more often than any other:
**"You must expand your money vessel."**

Of course, the original intention of the author who first preached this concept was likely not fatalistic determinism, but an excellent metaphor for needing to cultivate one's 'ability and capacity to handle money.' When I first started investing, I too thought this metaphor was a profound truth. I would comfort myself thinking, 'Ah, the reason my portfolio looks like this is because my vessel is still the size of a soy sauce dish. I need to read more books and control my mindset to grow this dish into a bowl, and eventually into a cauldron.'

However, as I rolled through the investment world, getting bruised and gaining real-world experience, I ran into the inherent limitations of this seemingly excellent 'vessel' metaphor.

Historically, in our daily lives, expressions like "having a large vessel" or "being a vessel meant for greatness" have been used to denote **'innate talent'** rather than acquired training. Because of this, when someone becomes wealthy, they are praised as "someone with an originally large vessel," but conversely, when someone fails at investing, their fierce efforts and potential to overcome limits are dismissed as a congenital lack of ability with the phrase, "That person inherently has a small money vessel." This creates a subtle sense of aversion and violence.

I am convinced that there is a far more appropriate and intuitive term to explain the modern mindset for real-world investing.
That is precisely the **'Money Muscle'**.

---

## The Hidden Shackles of the Word 'Vessel'

Have you ever been to a pottery studio? The moment you mold clay and bake it in a kiln, the size and shape of that vessel are permanently fixed.

**1. The Fixation of Limits**
The moment we compare someone's economic potential to a 'vessel,' we confine ourselves within a fixed space. We unconsciously draw a line, telling ourselves, "I am exactly this size of a person." When evaluated by others, it becomes even more cruel. It becomes a violent shackle that judges a failed person by saying, "I knew it. Your vessel is exactly that big."

**2. Sizing Up Human Value by Numbers**
What is more infuriating is that this 'vessel theory' sizes people up based solely on their current income and asset holdings. While money is undeniably important in a capitalist society, losing assets due to a single investment failure does not mean that a person's intellectual depth, philosophy, tenacity for the future, and value-creation ability have shrunk.

**3. The Loss of Opportunity Trapped in a Time Frame**
The most fatal flaw lies in 'time'. Those who preach about the money vessel eventually tell you that you "must grow the vessel." However, if we define ourselves by saying, 'My vessel is small right now,' we lose the courage to boldly increase our investment amounts or jump into new challenges immediately.
'My vessel is still small, so I'll wait until it gets big enough to make a large investment.'
What a foolish postponement! Growth happens in real-time as we crash into challenges and break down. By sitting still and waiting for our vessel to grow, we end up hopelessly missing the massive waves of opportunity flowing right before our eyes.

---

## Smash the Closed Bowl: Welcome to the 'Money Muscle'

Therefore, I want to propose that we boldly smash this outdated framework.
Our economic potential is not a static 'Container'. It is an organism that constantly receives stimuli and grows, much like our neural networks—it is the **'Money Muscle'**.

**How does a muscle grow?**
A vessel never knows its true limit until water overflows or it falls to the floor and shatters. But a muscle is different. You must lift a heavy barbell that is currently too much to handle, sweating and pushing yourself to the absolute limit, causing the muscle fibers to micro-tear. Only through the painful process of healing those torn wounds does the muscle finally grow larger and stronger.

The concept of the 'Money Muscle' does not allow us to wait. It awakens us to the painful truth that our economic capacity only expands when we actively shoulder heavy barbells (risks and challenges) and hit our limits *right now*.

In the past, I used to feel frustrated that no matter how passionately I explained good investment methods or mindsets to my acquaintances, extremely few ever put them into practice. In my early days, trapped in the 'money vessel theory,' I arrogantly misunderstood this, thinking, *"Ah, their vessel is still too small to fully contain my advice."*

But from the perspective of the 'Money Muscle theory,' it is interpreted entirely differently.
**Your muscles do not automatically grow just because someone beside you explains workout theories and spoon-feeds you know-how.** It's like how watching hundreds of deadlift videos on YouTube won't make your back any wider.

You must consistently and regularly go to the gym yourself, lift heavy barbells with proper form, sweat profusely, and use your own muscles to the limit before they finally grow and strengthen. You can never build your muscles with a barbell lifted by someone else.

Growth does not happen while sitting still, meditating, and imagining your soy sauce dish turning into a cauldron. It occurs in the vivid present moment when you endure the mind-crushing terror of heavy investment challenges, and when the wounds of failure and loss heal and recover.

---

## Step Out of the Kiln and Lift the Barbell

Perhaps the world wants us to remain as appropriately sized vessels. To live safely, holding only the steady water known as a salary, and quietly doing our part as cogs in the massive capitalist machine.

But we do not need to cram ourselves into the specifications molded by someone else or by society. We must wake up from the illusion of the 'money vessel,' which limits our potential with the one-dimensional yardsticks of income and assets.

Your financial potential is not baked pottery. It is a pulsating **muscle**, ready to tear, bleed, and grow stronger every single day.

Do not wait. Lift the barbell right now and tear through your limits.

**There is no money vessel. Only money muscle.** 🏋️‍♂️
