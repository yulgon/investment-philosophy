# [Theory] Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) Complete Guide: The Law of Market Risk and Return

## 1. What is the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)?

Earlier in [Evolution of Investment Chapter 1](/en/stay-the-course/006-evo-ch1) and **[Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)](/en/wiki/mpt)**, we learned the greatness of diversified investing. However, Harry Markowitz's MPT theory had a fatal flaw. It was practically impossible to calculate the correlations of hundreds of thousands of existing stocks one by one to create a perfect portfolio.

Then in 1964, William F. Sharpe appeared and proposed a great thought experiment that would change the investment world forever.
*"If all smart investors in the world were to make a perfect diversified investment as Markowitz suggested, where is the ultimate destination of the portfolio that people would end up owning?"*

Sharpe's answer was clear. **"That ultimate destination is exactly to own the entire stock market as a whole."**
The mathematical formulation of this massive realization is the flower of modern finance, the **Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)**.

## 2. Core Concepts of CAPM: Systematic Risk and Beta (β)

To fully understand CAPM, you must distinguish between the two types of 'Risk' that exist in the stock market.

### ① Unsystematic Risk
This is risk confined to a specific company or industry. For example, an event where Apple's new product fails or a fire breaks out at a Tesla factory. Sharpe saw that we can **perfectly erase this risk to '0' just by diversifying our investments across multiple stocks.** Since it is an erasable risk, no return compensation is given for it.

### ② Systematic Risk and Beta (β)
No matter how perfectly you diversify, the risk of a war breaking out or interest rates surging and causing the entire global economy to collapse can never be avoided. Sharpe called this unavoidable massive wave of the market **'Systematic Risk'**, and he named the indicator that measures this **'Beta (Beta, β)'**.
* **Beta = 1:** An asset that moves exactly like the market (S&P 500).
* **Beta > 1:** A dangerous asset that fluctuates more than the market (e.g., Tech stocks).
* **Beta < 1:** A defensive asset that fluctuates less than the market (e.g., Utility stocks).

## 3. The Truth of Practical Investing Revealed by the CAPM Formula

> **Expected Return = Risk-Free Rate + Beta × (Market Return - Risk-Free Rate)**

The message this seemingly complex formula sends to us retail investors is incredibly shocking and clear.
**"The reason we make more money investing in stocks than leaving it in a bank deposit (risk-free rate) is not because you are a genius at picking stocks, but solely as the price you pay for willingly bearing the terrifying risk of the market (Beta)."**

In other words, the effort to pick stocks (Alpha) is meaningless, and simply buying the entire market (Beta) and enduring it patiently is the only scientific principle that generates returns.

## 4. The Birth of the Index Fund and Connection to Subsequent Theories

William Sharpe's CAPM theory later became the perfect academic foundation for John Bogle to introduce **Index Funds (S&P 500 ETFs, etc.)** to the world. Vanguard's philosophy of "Don't look for the needle, buy the haystack" originated from this single formula.

And upon the foundation of this CAPM, an even more extreme and massive backbone theory was born, stating that "Since the market perfectly reflects all information already, no one can consistently beat the market return (Beta)." That is the eternal faith of index investors, the **[Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)](/en/wiki/emh)**.
